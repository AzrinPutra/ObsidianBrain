### Key principles of Usability

**Usability  is  defined  by  International  Organization  for  Standardization  (ISO)  as**
**“The  extent  to  which a  product  can  be used  by  specified  users to  achieve  specified**
**goals with effectiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction in a specified context of use”.**
**There are five main components which define usability:**
- **  Learnability – How easy is to perform basic tasks for new users?**
- **  Efficiency – What time does it take for users to find what they came for?**
- **  Memorability – How hard is it for users to repeatedly perform their tasks? **
- **  Error rate – Errors made by users.**
- **  Satisfaction – The comfort users feel when using the design.**

HCI Revision Guide: Core Concepts, Design, and Evaluation
**I. Fundamental Concepts of HCI**

|Term|Definition and Context|Source Citations|
|---|---|---|
|**HCI Definition**|The study and design of how people **engage with digital systems**, aiming to create technology that is intuitive, efficient, and enjoyable to use [1, 2]. It bridges human needs and technological possibilities [3, 4].|[1-4]|
|**ACM SIGCHI Model**|Describes HCI as the intersection of three core disciplines: **Computer Science (CS)** (ensures functionality and reliability) [5-7], **Human Factors (HF)**, or cognitive psychology (ensures the system fits human capabilities, focusing on perception, memory, cognition, motor skills) [5-7], and **Design (D)** (ensures aesthetics, clarity, and satisfaction) [7-9].|[5-9]|
|**The Computer**|A broad concept including **any computational system or digital technology** that processes information and responds to human inputs through an interface [10, 11]. This includes traditional computers, mobile devices, wearables, embedded systems (ATMs, kiosks), and AR/VR systems [12, 13].|[10-13]|
|**Interaction**|The **two-way communication (dialogue)** between humans and computers through an interface, involving humans providing input (gestures, commands) and computers providing output (feedback, results) [14-17].|[14-17]|
|**Design**|The **intentional process of creating solutions** to meet human needs, goals, and experiences, balancing functionality, usability, and aesthetics [18, 19].|[18, 19]|
- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**II. Cognitive Processes and Design Principles**
**A. Human Cognitive Processes**
Interacting with technology is a **cognitive process** [20]. Designers must account for cognitive limitations to design better interactive products [20].
- **Key Processes:** Attention, Perception, Memory, Learning, Problem-solving, planning, reasoning, and decision-making [21].
- **Perception:** How information is acquired from the world and transformed into experiences [22]. Good design must ensure representations are **readily perceivable** [22], meaning text should be legible and distinguishable from the background (colour contrast) [23].
- **Mental Models/Knowledge:** Users develop an understanding of a system, sometimes described as a **mental model**, which helps predict how to use the system or what to do in unfamiliar situations [24]. Users make **inferences** using these models [25].
**B. Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)**
CLT deals with the **mental effort required for learning** [26, 27].
- **Intrinsic Load:** The essential load based on the complexity of the content itself [28].
- **Extraneous Load:** Load caused by **poor instructional design** or presentation (e.g., cluttered layouts, unnecessary elements) [29-32]. Designers must strive to reduce this load [30].
- **Germane Load:** Load that supports **schema construction** and understanding [30, 31]. Strategies to enhance this include practice exercises and real-life examples [33].
**C. Core Design Principles**
These are generalizable abstractions for thinking about design [34-36].
- **Usability Goals:** Effectiveness, Efficiency, Safety, Utility, Learnability, and Memorability [37, 38].
- **Affordances:** Attributes of an object that allow people to know how to use it (e.g., a button invites pushing) [39-42]. Interfaces typically rely on *perceived* affordances [43].
- **Constraints:** Restricting the possible actions a user can perform to help prevent them from selecting incorrect options [44].
- **Consistency:** Designing operations and elements to be similar for similar tasks, making interfaces easier to learn and use (Internal vs. External consistency) [44, 45].
- **Feedback:** Immediate responses provided to users after interaction, assuring them their action was recognized [46, 47].
- **Visibility:** Making system status and options obvious to the user [35].
- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**III. Design Process and Deliverables**
**A. User-Centred Design (UCD) & Interaction Design (ID)**
HCI requires an early focus on the **Users and Tasks** [48, 49]. ID involves establishing requirements, developing alternatives, prototyping, and evaluating [48]. **Iteration is crucial** throughout the core activities [50, 51].
**B. Design Thinking (DT) Process**
DT is a methodology that is non-linear and solution-based, tackling complex problems by understanding human needs [52, 53].

|Stage|Goal|Key Activities/Deliverables|Source Citations|
|---|---|---|---|
|**1. Empathize**|Understand users, their needs, behaviors, and context [54-56].|Observation, Engagement (interviews, asking "Why?") [55-57].|[54-57]|
|**2. Define**|Bring clarity and focus to the design space by synthesizing findings [55, 56, 58].|Craft a meaningful and actionable Problem Statement or Point-of-View (POV) [59-61]. Techniques: User Personas, 5 Whys, HMW questions [61-63].|[55, 56, 58-60]|
|**3. Ideate**|Generate a **wide array of ideas**; process of "going wide" [55, 56, 64].|Concentrate on idea generation, encouraging divergent thinking and deferring judgment [64-66]. Techniques: Brainstorming, SCAMPER, Crazy 8s, Mind Mapping [67-69].|[55, 56, 64-66]|
|**4. Prototype**|Create tangible representations of solutions to **answer questions and gather feedback** [55, 56, 70, 71].|Building a simplified, low-resolution version quickly and cheaply [70, 71]. Fidelity types: Low-Fidelity (sketches, wireframes) [72], Medium-Fidelity [73], High-Fidelity (near final quality) [74, 75].|[55, 56, 70-72, 74]|
|**5. Test**|Solicit feedback on prototypes from users to **refine solutions and learn more about the user** [55, 56, 76].|Use testing to inform the next iterations [77]. A systematic, multi-method evaluation ensures iterative improvement [78-80].|[55, 56, 76, 77]|
**C. Key Deliverables**
- **Personas:** **Fictional characters, synthesized from real users** (not idealised) that capture a set of user characteristics, goals, and motivations [81-84].
- **Scenarios/Storyboards:** An **informal narrative story** or **visual storytelling technique** (like a comic strip) that maps a user's journey, illustrating how a user would perform tasks [82, 85]. Storyboarding helps build empathy and clarifies the idea [85].
- **Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA):** Analysis method focusing on **physical and observable actions** by breaking a task into subtasks and plans [86, 87].
- **Card Sorting:** Used early in design for **defining a system's architecture** by generating information about the associations and grouping of specific data items [88, 89].
- **Wireframes/UI Sketches:** Low-fidelity sketches focusing on **content organization, navigation flow, and functional priority**, avoiding distraction from aesthetics [90-92].

**IV. Usability Evaluation Methods (UEMs)**
Evaluation is performed **throughout design** to check requirements, usability, and user satisfaction [93]. Poor interfaces are a common reason for systems not being used and are a common cause of errors [94].
**A. Analytical (Expert-Based) Methods**
Rely on expert judgment [95-97].

|Method|Description|Advantages|Disadvantages|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Heuristic Evaluation**|Experts assess interface against established usability principles (heuristics) [95, 97-99].|Quick and inexpensive; identifies major issues early [98-101].|Subjective; may overlook real user behavior/context [99, 100, 102].|
|**Cognitive Walkthrough**|Experts simulate a **new user's** task completion step-by-step [99, 100, 102, 103].|Focuses on **learnability** for new users; highlights task flow problems [104-107].|Time-consuming for complex apps; does not capture satisfaction [104, 105].|
|**Guideline Review**|Experts check compliance with usability and accessibility guidelines (e.g., WCAG) [104, 105].|Ensures legal and accessibility standards are met [104, 105].|May miss nuanced issues that arise in real usage [104, 105].|
**B. Empirical (User-Based) Methods**
Involve real users interacting with the system [108, 109].

|Method|Description|Advantages|Disadvantages|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Moderated Usability Testing**|Users perform predefined tasks while observed [108, 109].|Direct insights into real user behavior (qualitative/quantitative); facilitates iterative improvement [108-110].|Resource-intensive; observer presence may influence behavior (Hawthorne effect) [108, 111].|
|**Remote Usability Testing**|Users complete tasks in their own environment using recording tools [111-113].|Tests app in real-life context; less expensive; broader reach (geographically diverse users) [111, 112, 114].|Limited observation/control over environment; may not capture subtle issues [111, 112, 115].|
|**A/B Testing**|Two or more versions of a UI element are tested to determine which performs better [116-119].|Provides quantitative data on effectiveness; ideal for testing specific UI changes [116-118, 120].|Requires large user sample for statistical significance; only tests specific features, not overall usability [121-124].|
|**Surveys/Questionnaires**|Users provide subjective feedback on satisfaction, ease of use, etc. (e.g., SUS) [116, 117, 125].|Collects perception and satisfaction data; easy to scale to many users [116, 117, 125, 126].|Does not reveal actual usage problems; subjective; limited depth of insight [116, 117, 127].|
|**Think-Aloud Protocol**|Users verbalize their thoughts and decisions while using the system [128-130].|Provides insight into users’ mental models and reasoning; immediate feedback on user understanding [129, 131].|Can be unnatural for users, altering behavior; time-intensive to analyze [129, 132, 133].|
|**Field Studies**|Observe users in their natural environment/context [134-136].|Provides realistic context and authentic behavior; uncovers contextual influences and unanticipated issues [137-139].|Time-consuming and costly; limited control over variables [137].|

**V. Advanced Topics & Real-World Failures**
**A. Accessibility and Universal Design**
**Accessibility** means designing interfaces so people of all abilities (visual, auditory, motor, cognitive) can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with systems [140, 141].
- **Universal Design:** A design approach that creates products usable by everyone, regardless of ability, aiming for inclusivity [142-145].
- **Universal Design Principles:** Equitable Use (usable by all without segregation) [142, 146], Flexibility in Use (accommodates a wide range of preferences/abilities) [142, 147], Simple and Intuitive Use [142, 148], Perceptible Information (communicates effectively regardless of sensory abilities) [149, 150], and Tolerance for Error [149, 151].
- **Assistive Technology (AT):** Hardware/software assisting users with disabilities, such as screen readers (JAWS, NVDA), speech recognition, sip-and-puff devices, and eye-tracking systems [152-156].
**B. Examples of Badly Designed Products**
Poor design typically results from a **lack of user-centered thinking, inadequate testing, or overcomplication** [157].

|Product/System|Poor Design Factor|Source Citations|
|---|---|---|
|**Windows 8 (2012)**|Mixed User Interfaces (jarring/confusing); removed the familiar Start button; too focused on touchscreens [158].|[158]|
|**Microsoft Zune (2006)**|Complex interface and failed syncing process compared to competitors [159, 160].|[159, 160]|
|**Samsung Galaxy Note 7 (2016)**|Critical design flaw (explosive battery) due to rushing ambitious features without adequate testing [161, 162].|[161, 162]|
|**Snapchat Redesign (2018)**|Confusing Navigation; alienated core audience, resulting in slower growth [163, 164].|[163, 164]|
|**Juicero (2016)**|Overcomplicated for a simple task; high price for little value, as pouches could be squeezed by hand [165].|[165]|
**C. Emerging Technologies (aligned with UN SDGs)**
Advanced HCI technologies are contributing to the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs):
- **AR/VR/MR:** Provide immersive simulations for **Quality Education (SDG 4)** and assist in surgical procedures for **Good Health (SDG 3)** [166, 167].
- **AI Chatbots:** Offer medical advice (SDG 3) and provide agricultural advice to farmers (SDG 2: Zero Hunger) [168, 169].
- **Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI):** Can empower people with disabilities, supporting **Reduced Inequalities (SDG 10)** [170].
- **Wearable Technology:** Tracks vital signs for **Good Health (SDG 3)** and monitors environmental conditions for **Decent Work (SDG 8)** [171, 172].
